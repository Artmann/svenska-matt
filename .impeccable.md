# Svenska Mått

## Design Context

### Users

Swedish home cooks following foreign recipes — mostly US/UK sites with cups,
ounces, °F and English ingredient names. They reach for the extension **mid
prep**, at a laptop in Chrome, wanting the ingredient list rewritten in familiar
Swedish measurements (dl/msk/tsk, grams, °C) and Swedish ingredient names, then
copied. The interaction is a few seconds inside a ~360px popup: open, glance,
maybe copy. Speed and legibility beat everything else.

### Brand Personality

Confident, Scandinavian, quietly precise. Three words: **clear, calm,
characterful**. It should feel like a well-made Nordic tool — trustworthy and
unfussy, with a distinct point of view rather than a generic utility popup. Voice
is plain, human Swedish; never cute, never corporate.

### Aesthetic Direction

Refined minimalism with a Scandinavian POV — this is an evolution of the current
clean look, not a maximalist rebuild. Generous, rhythmic whitespace; precise
typographic hierarchy; warm-tinted neutrals (no pure #000/#fff); a single
deliberate accent used only for the primary action and meaning. Supports **system
light/dark**. Anti-references: the AI-popup look (cyan-on-dark, purple gradients,
glassmorphism, drop-shadowed rounded cards everywhere), and sterile
utility-chrome. Memorable through restraint and detail, not decoration.

### Design Principles

1. **The list is the hero.** The converted Swedish ingredients get the most
   weight; the original English is a quiet secondary reference; app chrome
   recedes.
2. **Instant legibility.** Optimize for a 3-second glance while cooking — strong
   hierarchy, comfortable reading size, unmistakable section grouping.
3. **Confident restraint.** One accent, tinted neutrals, intentional spacing
   rhythm. Every element earns its place; nothing decorative.
4. **Tint everything, honor the system.** Neutrals lean toward the brand hue; no
   pure black/white; light and dark both feel deliberate.
5. **States that guide, in a human Swedish voice.** Loading, empty, and error
   states teach the next action (e.g. "select the list and try again") rather
   than just reporting.
